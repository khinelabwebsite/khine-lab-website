Background

The past decade saw a tremendous growth in the processing and wireless capabilities of electronic devices. As smart phones and computers become smaller and more powerful, people’s ability to be continuously connected to the internet and other devices has also become more possible. More importantly, this idea of connectivity, called the internet of things (IoT), now gives doctors the capability to wirelessly monitor patient’s health and recovery 24/7.

There are several key non-invasive vitals, such as pulse, blood pressure, temperature, and respiration, that doctors use to determine patient health. Continuous and remote monitoring of these vitals allows for earlier and better informed diagnosis, faster response to acute conditions, and the quantification of patient recovery. For example, continuous ECG monitoring give doctors the ability to see cardiac events that may not be present during a routine examination. Use of pressure sensors for continuous blood pressure monitor can alert physicians when the patient is having a stroke or is undergoing an ischemic event. Strain gauges and EMG sensors can be used to monitor and quantify patient activity and aid in faster rehabilitation.

Gaps in Current Knowledge

While current electronic hardware and software are more than capable of acquiring and sending data, the sensors used to detect these vitals needs to be further developed for wearable applications. Current industry standard sensors and electronics are planar, brittle, and limited in conformability. This makes them uncomfortable to wear and limits their detection capabilities. In order to maximize the capacity for acquiring body vitals and to increase comfort, wearable sensors must be able to match the highly flexible and dynamic environment of the human skin. The sensors themselves must be soft, flexible, and move well with strain and deformation of the body. They must also have a large dynamic range while maintaining a high signal to noise ratio.

Thus, there is still a need to develop highly conformal and sensitive sensors for patient vital monitoring that are comparable to current industry standards. The development and subsequent application of these sensors will improve patient monitoring, leading to faster diagnosis and response by doctors resulting in better patient care.
