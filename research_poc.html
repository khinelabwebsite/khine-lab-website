Background

Point-of-care (POC) diagnostics aim to shorten the gap between patients and medical tests by making sensitive diagnostic tests more affordable and accessible. Lower testing costs increase the likelihood of adopting testing methods in major healthcare systems across socioeconomic backgrounds. Accessibility to POC platforms allows patients to seek early diagnosis in areas with inconvenient or limited hospitals and laboratory facilities. Early diagnosis promotes faster treatment response . This is primarily important for time-sensitive conditions in which early treatment leads to more successful patient outcomes, such as heart attacks and stroke. Examples of existing POC devices include at-home pregnancy tests and diabetes glucose monitors.

Gaps in Current Knowledge

Commercially-available diagnostic tests are designed to yield high precision results. High precision is attained by using expensive laboratory-grade equipment and minimally-invasively collecting biofluids like blood with high concentrations of condition-specific biomarkers. These requirements raise the costs of healthcare services without significantly improving their convenience to patients and caregivers. Thus, there is a need to reduce testing costs and increase their convenience and availability.

POC diagnostics research aims to solve the problems of testing costs, accessibility, and poor sensitivity of low-costs methods. Improved sensitivity is pertinent for detecting diseases during early onset; reducing blood sample collection volumes; and using alternative, non-invasively collected biofluids like urine, saliva, and sweat that contain lower biomarker concentrations. Strategies for improving the sensitivity of POC tests include developing assay platforms with high signal output, such as multi-scale silica structures with enhanced fluorescence, or using superhydrophobic surfaces to concentrate urine biomarker tests to detectable levels. These platforms possess additional characteristics such as anti-bacteria, reduced blood coagulation, patternability, and multiplexity.

Current efforts research the development of colorimetric assays with open-channel, self-driven fluid flow.
